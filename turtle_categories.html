Turtle Categories

These are the Turtle Categories...

Class - Reptilia

Reptiles are a class of cold-blooded vertebrates - their body temperature varies with their environment. Reptiles include snakes, lizards, crocodiles, and turtles. Reptiles have scaly skin, breathe air with lungs, and have a three-chambered heart. Most reptiles lay eggs, although some produce eggs that hatch internally.

Order - Testudines

This order includes all turtles and tortoises. It is divided into 3 suborders: Pleurodira (side-necked turtles), Cryptodira (freshwater turtles, snapping turtles, tortoises, soft-shelled turtles, and sea turtles), and Amphichelydia (a suborder of turtles that is now extinct).

Suborder - Cryptodira

This suborder includes sea turtles, freshwater turtles, snapping turtles, tortoises, and soft-shelled turtles.

Families

Most scientists recognize 2 families of sea turtles:

Family Cheloniidae includes all sea turtles with scutes (horny plates) covering their shells.

Family Dermochelyidae are scute-less turtles with only 1 modern species; the leatherback turtle. A leatherback sea turtle is covered with leathery skin. It is the only marine turtle whose backbone is not attached to the inside of its shell.

Genus, species

Most scientists recognize 7 species and 1 subspecies of sea turtles:

Green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas mydas).

black or Eastern Pacific green turtle (Chelonia mydas agassizii).

Loggerhead (Caretta caretta).

flatback (Natator depressus)

leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea).

Turtle Categories in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is home to five turtle species, categorized based on their conservation status. The critically endangered Hawksbill turtle and the endangered Green turtle are commonly found. The Olive Ridley turtle and Loggerhead turtle are classified as vulnerable, while the Leatherback turtle is considered endangered. Conservation efforts in Sri Lanka focus on protecting nesting beaches, reducing threats, and raising awareness to ensure the survival of these remarkable marine creatures.

Hikkaduwa Beach

Hikkaduwa Beach, located on the southwestern coast of Sri Lanka, is known for its beautiful sandy shores and vibrant marine life. While sea turtle sightings cannot be guaranteed, it is possible to encounter several turtle species in the waters of Hikkaduwa Beach. The most commonly observed turtle species in this area are:

Green turtle (Chelonia mydas): Green turtles are frequently spotted in the waters around Hikkaduwa Beach. They are known for their large size and herbivorous diet.

Hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata): Hawksbill turtles are known for their unique and beautiful shell patterns. They can occasionally be seen in the waters near Hikkaduwa Beach.

Loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta): Loggerhead turtles are less common but may also be encountered in the waters of Hikkaduwa Beach. They are known for their powerful jaws and broad heads.

Rekawa Beach

Rekawa Beach, located on the southeastern coast of Sri Lanka, is renowned as a significant nesting site for sea turtles. Several turtle species visit Rekawa Beach for nesting. The most commonly observed turtle species in Rekawa Beach are:

Olive Ridley turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea): Rekawa Beach is famous for its Olive Ridley turtle nesting activity. Thousands of Olive Ridley turtles come ashore during their nesting season, which typically occurs from April to July.

Green turtle (Chelonia mydas): Green turtles also nest on Rekawa Beach, although in smaller numbers compared to Olive Ridley turtles. These majestic turtles can be spotted during the nesting season.

Loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta): Loggerhead turtles occasionally visit Rekawa Beach for nesting, though their presence is relatively less frequent compared to Olive Ridley and Green turtles.

Fossil Record

The first turtles appeared during the Triassic period, 245 to 209 million years ago. The earliest known sea turtles appear in the fossil record in the Early Cretaceous period, about 110 million years ago. Scientists believe that modern sea turtles are derived from marsh-inhabiting ancestors that lived during the Late Triassic period. Fossil records show that the now-extinct sea turtle Archelon ischyros, which lived 75 to 65 million years ago, was one of the largest turtles that ever lived. It reached a length of 4.6 m (15 ft.) and weighed more than 2,700 kg (6,000 lbs.). Together with saltwater crocodiles, sea snakes, and marine iguanas, sea turtles are the only surviving seawater-adapted reptiles.
